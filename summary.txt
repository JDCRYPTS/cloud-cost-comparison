AWS vs Azure: Provider Justification Summary

Scenario A: Microsoft-Centric Enterprise

For a business already using Microsoft products such as 
Windows Server, SQL Server, Microsoft 365, or Active 
Directory, Azure is the more cost-effective choice. 

Our testing showed that enabling Azure Hybrid Benefit 
on a Windows VM reduced the monthly cost from $137.24 
to $70.08, a saving of $67.16 per month or $805.92 per 
year on a single server. This benefit is available to 
any business that already owns Microsoft licenses through 
a Volume Licensing agreement, meaning they are not paying 
for something new but simply reusing what they already own.

Beyond cost, Azure integrates natively with Microsoft 
Entra ID for identity management, connects seamlessly 
with Microsoft 365 services, and provides strong support 
for .NET applications. For a Microsoft-stack enterprise, 
the total cost of ownership is lower on Azure when all 
integration and licensing factors are considered, not 
just the raw compute price.

Scenario B: Linux-Based Startup

For a startup running an open-source stack such as 
Node.js, Python, or PHP on Linux, AWS is the stronger 
choice. Our estimates showed AWS coming in at $46.05 
per month compared to Azure at $196.75 per month for 
a comparable setup, a significant difference for a 
budget-conscious startup.

AWS also offers a 12-month Free Tier that allows new 
businesses to test and iterate at minimal cost during 
early development. Its broader global infrastructure, 
larger marketplace of pre-configured solutions, and 
more mature open-source ecosystem make it the practical 
choice for teams building on Linux.

Conclusion

Neither provider is universally better. The right choice 
depends entirely on the business context. Microsoft-based 
enterprises benefit more from Azure while Linux-based 
startups benefit more from AWS.
